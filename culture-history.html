History

Walk through centuries of German history — from the Holy Roman Empire to reunification.

A Brief History of Germany

Germany has one of the most complex and fascinating histories in the world. From the ancient Germanic tribes and the Holy Roman Empire to the turbulent 20th century and the remarkable story of reunification, the country's past shapes everything you see when you visit today.

Key Historical Periods

  • Ancient Germanic Tribes (before 500 AD) — Germanic peoples settled the region and resisted Roman expansion. The Battle of Teutoburg Forest in 9 AD halted Rome's advance east.
  • Holy Roman Empire (800–1806) — Charlemagne united much of Western Europe and was crowned Emperor in 800 AD. The Empire lasted over 1,000 years.
  • The Reformation (1517) — Martin Luther nailed his 95 Theses to the church door in Wittenberg, sparking a religious revolution that transformed Europe.
  • German Unification (1871) — Prussia's Otto von Bismarck united the German states into the German Empire under Kaiser Wilhelm I.
  • World War I (1914–1918) — Germany's defeat led to the fall of the Kaiser and the signing of the Treaty of Versailles.
  • Weimar Republic and Rise of Hitler (1919–1933) — A fragile democracy gave way to the Nazi regime under Adolf Hitler.
  • World War II and the Holocaust (1939–1945) — The most devastating conflict in history, resulting in the murder of six million Jews and millions of others.
  • Division and Cold War (1945–1989) — Germany was divided into East and West. The Berlin Wall was built in 1961 and fell on November 9, 1989.
  • Reunification (1990) — East and West Germany were officially reunited on October 3, 1990 — now celebrated as German Unity Day.

Top Historical Sites

Wittenberg

The birthplace of the Protestant Reformation. Visit the Castle Church where Martin Luther posted his famous 95 Theses in 1517.

Berlin Wall Memorial

A preserved section of the Berlin Wall with a comprehensive memorial documenting the history of German division from 1961 to 1989.

Dachau Memorial

The site of the first Nazi concentration camp, now a memorial and museum dedicated to the victims of the Holocaust.
